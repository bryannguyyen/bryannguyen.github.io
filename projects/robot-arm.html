5-DOF Robotic Arm

Role: Mechanical + Firmware • Tools: SolidWorks, Arduino/C++ • Jul 2025 – Present

Overview: Teleoperated 5-DOF arm with NEMA-17 base and servo joints. Goal was smooth real-time control from a master potentiometer rig and a clean, serviceable mechanical design.

Design & CAD

5-DOF articulated arm: a 360° swiveling base plus shoulder, elbow, and wrist joints (each up to ~180° travel). The gripper (end effector) is modular and can be swapped to suit different tasks.

  • Joints actuated by a hybrid scheme: stepper motor for base rotation; hobby servo actuation for shoulder, elbow, and wrist.
  • Most structural components are 3D-printed (PLA, ABS, and PA6-GF). PA6-GF and ABS are used at high-load interfaces and for gears; PLA is used for non-critical covers and brackets.
  • All fasteners standardized to M3 hardware for simplicity and serviceability.
  • Electronics are enclosed in the side compartment for clean routing and easy maintenance.
  • Full assembly modeled in SolidWorks; tolerances tuned for press-fit inserts and repeatable assembly.

Electronics & Control

  • Custom wiring harness and power rails, hand-wired and soldered on perfboard for robustness and field repair.
  • Power: 12 V, 10 A DC barrel-jack supply.
  • Two 8 A buck converters step down voltage rails for the different loads (servos vs. stepper/logic).
  • Controller: Arduino Nano + stepper driver. The base stepper is micro-stepped for smooth rotation; servos are commanded with rate-limited setpoints.
  • Teleoperation via a “master arm” populated with potentiometers; real-time readings are mapped to joint angles on the puppet arm.

Signal conditioning: to reduce jitter from the potentiometers, a small software deadband (~2–3°) is applied around the neutral reading, preventing tiny fluctuations from commanding the servos.

Testing & Results

  • Reach: ~14 in (fully extended).
  • Payload: up to ~250 g at the end effector in nominal postures.
  • End effectors: quick-swap interface supports grippers or task-specific tools.
  • Teleoperation: master arm mapping verified for smooth, low-latency control of shoulder, elbow, and wrist.

Challenges & Iterations

  • Gear and horn failures: early PLA gears and servo horns stripped under load. Fixed by (1) changing print orientation so shear does not align with the weakest layer lines and (2) switching materials to ABS and PA6-GF for gears/structural parts.
  • Power headroom: the initial 12 V/4 A supply sagged during multi-axis moves. Upgrading to a 12 V/10 A supply and routing thicker-gauge rails on the perfboard eliminated brownouts.
  • Signal noise/jitter: raw potentiometer readings caused servo twitch. A software deadband (~2–3°) around neutral and gentle rate limiting smoothed motion substantially.

Next Steps

  • Inverse-kinematics & playback: record master-arm motion and play it back on the puppet arm with IK-based joint planning.
  • Filtering & shielding: add simple moving-average filtering or RC filters and twisted-pair/shielded runs for the potentiometer bundle.
  • Hardened drive components: evaluate machined or molded gears for long-term durability.
